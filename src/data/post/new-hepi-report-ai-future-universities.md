<p>As I sit-down to write the first blog on behalf of our new&nbsp;company,&nbsp;WorkSmart-AI, my attention has&nbsp;just&nbsp;been&nbsp;drawn&nbsp;towards&nbsp;a new report by HEPI,&nbsp;<a href="https://www.hepi.ac.uk/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/AI-and-the-Future-of-Universities.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer noopener">AI-and-the-Future-of-Universities.pdf.</a>&nbsp;Whilst there are many facts and thoughts that I could highlight from this incredibly interesting series of essays, my particular favourite&nbsp;(I’m&nbsp;unsurprisingly biased given the article’s focus on staff development)&nbsp;is contained within&nbsp;Professor&nbsp;Janice Kay and Derfel Owen’s essay addressing the &#8220;Opportunities and challenges for the higher education workforce&#8221;.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<p>In it they write:&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote is-layout-flow wp-block-quote-is-layout-flow">
<p><em>“In a robust culture of AI literacy, effective&nbsp;staff&nbsp;development is a strategic imperative&#8230;&#8230; We must shift from gradual adaptation to institution-wide workforce planning, ensuring that staff are competent in using AI and equipped to engage critically with implications for pedagogy,&nbsp;assessment&nbsp;and academic integrity. Proactive workforce planning that embeds AI competency across the staff base must be a core institutional priority.</em>&nbsp;</p>
</blockquote>

<p>This need to&nbsp;empower&nbsp;University staff&nbsp;with the time and the opportunities to carry out staff development,&nbsp;in&nbsp;order to be able to embrace and not fear AI,&nbsp;is something that Dave Weller&nbsp;(my new business partner!) and I are passionate about &#8211; and is the reason that we’ve&nbsp;created&nbsp;WorkSmart-AI.&nbsp;</p>

<p>We’ve&nbsp;seen first-hand the huge benefits and genuine excitement that well-trained and supported staff can feel when using AI in&nbsp;the&nbsp;workplace. However,&nbsp;we’ve&nbsp;also&nbsp;met lots of colleagues across the sector who have not been so lucky in receiving&nbsp;any form of&nbsp;training,&nbsp;and who are genuinely terrified with what AI could mean to their jobs&nbsp;and their careers.&nbsp;</p>

<p>For many staff, it’s not a lack of interest or enthusiasm that is holding them back from grabbing the&nbsp;&#8216;AI gauntlet&#8217;, but rather the lack of time for training, and&nbsp;access to&nbsp;information as to what are the &#8216;do’s and&nbsp;don&#8217;ts&#8217; when it comes to their usage of it.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Throw in a multitude of other reasons (increasing volume of work / decreasing&nbsp;budgets)&nbsp;and&nbsp;it’s&nbsp;totally understandable&nbsp;to see that unless time for AI training is specifically added to calendars and strategically planned for, then&nbsp;despite all the good intentions,&nbsp;it will&nbsp;never materialise.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Having worked in the HE sector for almost a decade, I totally appreciate that there are so many challenges facing the sector currently&#8230;but as&nbsp;Kay and Owen state at the end of their essay:&nbsp;</p>

<blockquote class="wp-block-quote is-layout-flow wp-block-quote-is-layout-flow">
<p><em>“The question is not whether universities and their senior leaders and governors can afford to invest in AI transformation, but whether they can&nbsp;afford the consequences of failing to do so.”</em>&nbsp;</p>
</blockquote>
