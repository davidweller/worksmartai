Module 6: Prompting Basics - The R-C-T Framework

Why Prompts Matter

Rubbish In, Rubbish Out

Remember the parrot analogy from Module 2? If you ask a parrot to "Say something," it might squawk random noise. If you ask it to "Recite the first line of Hamlet," it knows exactly which words to predict.

If you do the same with AI, that is usually not the AI's fault. It is a "prompting" fault.

The quality of the AI's output is directly tied to the specific details in your input.

This module teaches you a simple framework - R-C-T - that transforms mediocre prompts into effective ones.

The R-C-T Framework

You do not need to be a software engineer to get good results. You just need a simple structure. The R-C-T Framework has three components: Role, Context, Task.

The R-C-T Framework

Think of it as briefing a new colleague. You would not just say "write something about deadlines." You would explain who they are writing as, what the situation is, and exactly what you need from them.

R - Role: Who Is the AI Today?

The Role sets the AI's perspective, expertise, and voice.

Why It Matters

Without a role, the AI defaults to "generic helpful assistant" - which produces generic, undifferentiated output. Assigning a role activates specific patterns from the AI's training:

  • "Act as a strict academic editor" → Critical, precise, focused on errors
  • "Act as a supportive student advisor" → Warm, encouraging, solution-focused
  • "Act as a concise executive" → Brief, direct, action-oriented

Role Examples

Your task

Effective role

Proofreading an assessment brief

"Act as a meticulous academic editor who catches ambiguity and unclear instructions"

Drafting a student-facing email

"Act as a friendly but professional Student Services advisor"

Simplifying a complex policy

"Act as a plain-English specialist who makes bureaucratic text accessible"

Generating discussion questions

"Act as an experienced seminar leader in [subject area]"

Writing a reference letter

"Act as a supportive academic referee who highlights strengths professionally"

Creating meeting agenda

"Act as an efficient administrator who values people's time"

C - Context: What Does the AI Need to Know?

The Context provides the background information the AI cannot guess.

Why It Matters

Remember: AI is a prediction engine, not a mind reader. It does not know:

  • Who your audience is
  • What has happened before
  • What constraints you are working within
  • What your institution's culture is like
  • What outcome you actually need

If you do not provide this, the AI will guess - and guessing means generic.

The Four Context Questions

When preparing a prompt, ask yourself:

Question

What to include

Who is this for?

The audience: students, colleagues, external partners, senior leadership

What is the situation?

Relevant background: what happened, what is happening, what is coming

What are the constraints?

Length limits, tone requirements, format needs, things to avoid

What does success look like?

The goal: to inform, persuade, reassure, instruct, celebrate

Context Examples

Weak context:

"Write an email about assignment extensions."

Strong context:

"The audience is first-year undergraduate students who are new to university and may be anxious about asking for help. The situation is that we have a new online system for requesting extensions, launching next week. The email should be reassuring (not bureaucratic), under 200 words, and must include the link to the new system."

The difference: The weak version could produce anything. The strong version guides the AI toward exactly what you need.

T - Task: What Exactly Do You Want?

The Task specifies the output format and any structural requirements.

Why It Matters

"Write something about X" invites the AI to choose the format. It might give you:

  • An essay when you wanted bullet points
  • A formal letter when you wanted a casual message
  • 500 words when you needed 100
  • A list when you needed flowing prose

Being explicit about the form of the output saves editing time.

While you're writing exactly what you want the AI to do (your task), here are some form items to consider including, to help the AI give you the best result possible:

Examples

Format

"Write a bullet-point list" / "Create a table" / "Draft a three-paragraph email"

Length

"In exactly 100 words" / "No more than one page" / "A brief summary (2-3 sentences)"

Structure

"Use headings for each section" / "Start with the key action, then explain why"

Tone

"Keep the tone encouraging" / "Be direct and professional"

Inclusions

"Include a clear call to action" / "End with next steps" / "Mention the deadline"

Exclusions

"Do not include jargon" / "Avoid passive voice" / "Do not assume prior knowledge"

The R-C-T Framework in Practice

Worked Example: The Makeover

Without R-C-T: "Write an email about the library renovation." The AI will write a generic, boring email. It might invent dates. It might be too formal or too casual. It has no idea if the library is closing for a day or a year.

With R-C-T: "[Role] Act as the Head Librarian. [Context] We are closing the main floor for painting next week (Monday-Wednesday). The quiet study rooms remain open. The audience is stressed students preparing for exams. [Task] Draft a short, reassuring email notifying them of the closure. Emphasise that study spaces are still available. Keep it under 200 words."

The R-C-T prompt produces a specific, empathetic, and accurate draft that requires very little editing. The difference is not the AI - it is the quality of the instruction.

Activity: Prompting Lab

You are an administrator with messy notes from a "Welcome Week" planning meeting. You need to turn them into a clear agenda for the next meeting. Write a prompt using the R-C-T framework. A strong answer would include: Role ("Act as an efficient Meeting Coordinator"), Context ("I have pasted rough notes below from our planning session. The team is overwhelmed, so we need clarity"), and Task ("Summarise these notes into a structured meeting agenda for next week. Use clear headings and bold the action items").

The Follow-Up (Iterating)

Even with R-C-T, the first result is not always perfect. Do not give up. Treat the AI like an intern - if the intern brings you a draft that is too long, you would not fire them. You would say: "Good start, but make it shorter." Common follow-up prompts include: "Make it more professional," "That is too long - summarise it in 3 bullet points," "Change the tone to be friendlier," and "Format that as a table."

Summary

The quality of your AI output is determined by the quality of your input. The R-C-T Framework gives you a simple, memorable structure: Role (who the AI should be), Context (the background information), and Task (exactly what you want). Iterate when needed - treat the first response as a draft, not a final answer.
